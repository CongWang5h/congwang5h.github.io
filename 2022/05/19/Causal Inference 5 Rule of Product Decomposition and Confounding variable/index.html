Randomized controlled experiment

The ultimate aim of many studies is to predict the effect of interventions on some outcomes. i.e. the causal relationship between two variables.

  • Do taller people make more money?
  • Do magnets help relieve pain?

The treatment variable is the one that may cause the difference in the outcome variable.

  • Do taller people make more money?
    Treatment: Height
    Outcome: Income

  • Do magnets help relieve pain?
    Treatment: Magnets
    Outcome: Pain

Confounding variable(confounder)

The variable

  • Affects the outcome variable.
  • Affects the treatment variable.

Example: Does exercise prevent a cold?

  • For a sample of subjects, record the amount of exercise per week and the number of colds over a year. Suppose you find that exercise is correlated with fewer colds.
    • Treatment?
    • Outcome?
    • List of possible confounders?

Treatment : exercise
Outcome: colds
Confounders: weather, age, occupation, …

Using a randomized experiment, we can remove the effect of the confounders.

Observational vs Experimental

  • Observational study
    • data are observed and collected on each subject.
    • No manipulation of the subject’s environment occurs.
  • Experiment
    • Manipulate the subjects’ environment.
    • Measure the outcome.

Example: Does exercise prevent a cold?

  • Observational study
    • Randomly select a sample of the subject.
    • Record data for each subject on amount of exercise and number of colds last year.
  • Randomized Experiment
    • Obtain a group of study participants(often volunteers)
    • Intervention: randomly assign the participants to the treatment(exercise) and control (no confounder).
    • After a set amount of time, record the amount of exercise and the number of colds for each person.

e.g. A/B test

An observational study may reveal the correlation between two variables, but only a randomized experiment can directly prove cause and effect.

  • Confounding may be present in the observational study.
  • Randomized assignment to treatment and control groups in an experiment makes all other factors that influence the outcome vary at random, so any change in outcome is attributable to the treatment.

Principles of Good Experiment

  • Control group
    • Subjects in this group do not receive the treatment but may receive a placebo.
    • So we can tell what happens to the outcome without the treatment (baseline)
  • Randomization
    • Random assignment to treatment and control groups.
    • It helps to equalize groups with respect to cofounders
  • Double blinding
    • Neither the subject nor the researchers evaluating them know who is in the treatment and control groups
    • Helps prevent bias
  • Placebo
    • Fake treatment, sugar pill
    • Help control the placebo effect: people who believe they are getting treatment often get better even if the treatment has no active ingredient.
    • Study shows placebo helps 62%62\%62% of headache sufferers, 58%58\%58% of those with sea-sickness.

Placebo and blinding are not always possible.

If observational studies can’t prove cause and effect, why don’t researcher always do randomized experiments?
E.g. left/right-handness and mathematical aptidude, height and income.
